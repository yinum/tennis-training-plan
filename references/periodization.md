# Periodization for recreational and club tennis

Adapted from classic sports periodization to the reality of adult club players:
limited court time, no off-season, skills gated by technique more than fitness.
The plan's honesty is its value — an over-promised plan gets abandoned in week 3.

## Realistic progression expectations (anchor every plan on these)

| From → To (NTRP) | Typical timeline at 2–3 focused sessions/week | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 2.5 → 3.0 | 3–6 months | fastest gains anywhere on the ladder |
| 3.0 → 3.5 | 6–12 months | split step + rally tolerance do most of the work |
| 3.5 → 4.0 | 1–2 seasons | requires a serve and a reliable second wing |
| 4.0 → 4.5 | 1–3 years | most adults plateau here; match play becomes essential |
| 4.5 → 5.0+ | multi-year, often never | needs junior-development-grade volume |

Rules that follow: a macrocycle (8–12 weeks) targets **skills**, not a band jump.
Frame the goal as "close the two gates the report identified" — the rating follows.
One session/week maintains; improvement starts at two; three+ accelerates.

## Structure

- **Macrocycle (8–12 weeks)** — one theme built from the report's top 2–3
  development priorities (never more). Ends with re-assessment: record a new video,
  re-run the analysis skill, compare radar/trend.
- **Mesocycles (3–4 weeks each, usually 3)**:
  1. **Acquisition** — new movement patterns in low-pressure conditions (drop feeds,
     shadow, wall). High rep, low decision load. Blocked practice dominates.
  2. **Consolidation** — same patterns under time pressure and movement (live feeds,
     ball machine, cooperative rally constraints). Variable practice dominates.
  3. **Integration** — patterns under match conditions (point play with constraints,
     practice sets, league matches). Random practice dominates. Includes a light
     final week before re-assessment.
  Each mesocycle has explicit **exit criteria** taken from the report's measurable
  success metrics; advance only when met, extend a week (once) when not, and if
  still unmet, simplify the drill level rather than pushing through.
- **Microcycle (1 week)** — a concrete schedule table respecting the player's real
  availability. Every session: 10 min warm-up (dynamic + shadow patterns), core
  block (the phase's focus drills), 10–15 min play/serve tail, cool-down note.

## Load and safety rules

- Volume/intensity increases ≤ ~10% per week; after 3 build weeks, 1 lighter week.
- Technique changes temporarily lower performance — warn the player in the plan
  ("your forehand will feel worse for two weeks; that's the pattern rewiring").
- Existing injuries: substitute, don't push through (e.g., shoulder issues → half
  serve volume, more return work). Flag anything that warrants a professional.
- Schedule at most as many sessions as the player committed to; a 3-session plan
  for a 2-session life is a broken plan.

## Practice-design principles

- **Blocked → variable → random** across the macrocycle (matches mesocycle order).
- Every drill gets a **success metric** (count/percentage/target zone), so the
  player knows when to level up — progression is criterion-based, not calendar-based.
- Pair every weakness drill with an **asset drill** from the player's specialties:
  plans that only grind weaknesses kill motivation.
- Deliberate practice ≠ playing sets: cap free play at ~1/3 of court time during
  acquisition, raising toward 2/3 by integration.
- No court? Wall work, shadow swings with video self-check, footwork ladders, and
  serve-toss practice all count and belong in the schedule.

## Re-assessment loop

End every macrocycle with: "record 20+ minutes of similar footage (same camera
angle if possible) and re-run tennis-video-analysis". The next plan starts from the
new report's radar-vs-radar diff. State this explicitly in the plan document.
